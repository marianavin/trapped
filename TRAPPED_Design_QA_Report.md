# TRAPPED — Design QA Report

Scope: the active `trapped/` build (shell + Level 3 + Level 4). `trapped-game/` was left out per your call — it has no shell/auth and is the earlier iteration. Findings are grounded in the actual source (file and line references throughout) and checked against your own `TRAPPED_Game_Design_Blueprint.md` and `TRAPPED_Visual_Sound_Reference.md`, since those set the bar this build should be hitting.

The short version: the shell (login, hub, level select) is genuinely well built for accessibility — better than most production apps, with real ARIA roles, focus-visible rings, and one correctly-implemented screen-reader announcement pattern in the dispatcher call. But that pattern isn't applied consistently, the reveal screens — the moment the blueprint calls "the biggest tension payoff — protect it" — use a color palette that fails contrast at the font sizes actually shipped, and the visual craft is currently pure Tailwind color blocks and monospace text with zero illustration, sprite, or texture, which is most of why it reads as flat despite the retro-pixel ambition in the reference doc.

## Accessibility

### Critical — breaks the experience for a real class of players

**The reveal screen's own color system fails contrast.** I measured the exact hex values from your spec's Reveal Screen table (`#2ECC71` escaped-green and `#E74C3C` caught-red on `#F5F5F5` background) against WCAG 2.1: escaped-green comes out at 1.93:1 and caught-red at 3.50:1, against a 4.5:1 requirement for normal text (both colors run at 10–12px pixel font in the actual components — `BiasCard.jsx:24`, `RevealScreen.jsx:16`, `LevelSelect.jsx:30`, `ProgressView.jsx:55`, `level3/Reveal.jsx:70`). This isn't an implementation slip — the failing colors are specified in `TRAPPED_Visual_Sound_Reference.md`'s own Reveal Screen table, so it's a design-system issue that has propagated into both levels' reveal and progress screens. Practically: a meaningful fraction of players — anyone with even mild low vision, or just a phone screen in bright expo lighting — will struggle to read whether they escaped or fell for a bias on the single screen the whole game is built to pay off. Fix: darken both toward something like `#1E8449` (green) and `#C0392B` (red) at this background, or drop the background lightness, and re-check with a contrast tool before locking it in again. The locked-level card treatment (`text-l4text/30` on `bg-l4panel/40`, `LevelSelect.jsx:21`) has the same problem at 2.39:1 — barely legible "LOCKED" state.

**Level 4's ground truth is entirely non-visual-alternative-free.** The 8-second flash scene (`FlashScene.jsx`) — the only ground truth the player gets, deliberately unpausable and unreplayable per the design intent — is built as animated colored `<div>`s (a car, a pedestrian, a streetlight) with no text, caption, or audio description of what actually happened. A blind or low-vision player has no way to perceive the "truth" the rest of the level is testing their memory against, so the entire bias mechanic (which depends on contradicting something you saw) can't function for them. This is a real design tension, not just an oversight — the mechanic's whole point is that it's fast and visual — but it's worth a deliberate decision (e.g., an optional audio description track, or accepting and documenting this as a sighted-only mechanic) rather than an accidental gap.

### High — inconsistent, will bite real users

**Screen-reader announcements exist in exactly one place.** `DispatcherCall.jsx:44-60` has a genuinely well-built pattern: a separate `sr-only` `aria-live="polite"` region announces each question in full the instant it arrives, decoupled from the visual typewriter effect (so a screen reader isn't forced to listen to a letter-by-letter reveal). That's the right solution. It just isn't used anywhere else. `Act1Location.jsx`, `Act2Keypad.jsx`, `Setup.jsx` (level 3), `SetupScreen.jsx`, `ConsequenceScreen.jsx` (level 4), and the hub's tab-switching in `HubScreen.jsx` all change screen content with no live-region announcement and no focus movement to the new content. A screen-reader user moving through Level 3 gets essentially silence between the one moment (dispatcher call in Level 4) where someone remembered to wire this up. Recommend extracting the `DispatcherCall` pattern into a shared hook (e.g. `useAnnounce(text)`) and applying it at every stage transition across both levels.

**The scrambled keypad has no visible focus indicator.** The `PixelButton` component (`PixelButton.jsx:29`) has a proper `focus-visible:outline` ring and it's used consistently everywhere else. `Act2Keypad.jsx:94-104` builds its number buttons as raw `<button>` elements instead of `PixelButton`, and they carry no focus ring at all. For a keyboard-only player, tabbing through a 3×4 grid of unlabeled-by-position digits with zero visual focus indicator makes it close to unplayable — the same button in `Act1Location.jsx:62` (the sign tap) has the identical gap.

**No motion-reduction support anywhere.** There is no `prefers-reduced-motion` media query in `index.css` or any component. The CRT flicker (`crtFlicker`, 9s infinite loop, `index.css:47`), the pulsing "waiting for help" voice text (`Act2Keypad.jsx:115-124`, infinite 2.2s opacity pulse), the emergency-button-style pulses, and every Framer Motion transition play at full intensity regardless of a player's OS-level motion preference. For an expo setting where you don't control who's playing, this is worth a one-line global override (respect `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` by disabling the infinite/ambient animations, keeping the discrete transitions).

### Medium

Body copy runs at 8–11px Press Start 2P (`text-[8px]` through `text-[11px]`, e.g. `LevelSelect.jsx:29`, `BiasCard.jsx:20`) combined with global `-webkit-font-smoothing: none` (`index.css:18`) and all-caps UI copy throughout. Individually each is an intentional retro-aesthetic choice from the reference doc; together, on a phone at an expo, this is genuinely hard to read for anyone without sharp near vision. Worth testing at arm's length on an actual phone before locking sizes in — a few px of headroom on body/label text won't break the aesthetic.

`body { overflow: hidden; user-select: none }` (`index.css:16-20`) blocks text selection and clips any content taller than the viewport. That's a deliberate "feels like a system" choice, but it also means a player who pinch-zooms (a very common low-vision accommodation) can end up with unreachable, clipped content, since nothing can scroll to compensate outside the few screens that explicitly opt into `overflow-y-auto`.

## Engagement & visual craft

The honest note here: the writing and mechanics are the strongest part of this build by a wide margin — the leading-question design in `data/level4.js`, the anchoring trap in `level3/data.js`, and the scoring logic are sharp and exactly on-brief. The visual layer hasn't caught up to them yet, and that's almost certainly what's reading as "not engaging."

There are zero images, icons, sprites, or SVGs anywhere in `src/` — every visual element, including the car and pedestrian in the Level 4 flash scene and every button and card across both levels, is a plain Tailwind-colored `<div>` or bordered `<button>`. Your own `TRAPPED_Visual_Sound_Reference.md` calls for "chunky pixel art... every element reads instantly," referencing Pokémon and Game Boy-era sprite work. What's shipped instead is closer to a color-coded wireframe: the "car" in `FlashScene.jsx:61-74` is a single `14×6` rounded rectangle, the "keypad" in `Act2Keypad.jsx` is a plain bordered grid of digits. It's functional and legible, but it doesn't yet look like a game — it reads as a prototype. Since your stack already includes Framer Motion and you're generating chiptune audio programmatically (`audio/synth.js`), the cheapest craft upgrade is probably a small set of actual pixel sprites (even 16×16 or 32×32 hand-pixeled PNGs, image-rendering: pixelated is already set globally) for the handful of recurring visual beats — the car, the pedestrian, a phone/keypad frame, the dispatcher — rather than continuing to build scenes out of colored boxes.

Every screen in both levels uses the same layout shape: centered column, dark background, pixel-font heading, mono body text, bordered button. That's consistent with "no decorative elements," but it also means Level 3 and Level 4 are visually almost indistinguishable outside their accent color, even though the blueprint asks for each level to have "its own palette" and feel distinct enough that "minute three should feel different from minute one." Nothing currently differentiates the *texture* of a bomb-defusal panel from a dispatcher call — both are a text box and some buttons.

Per your own Feedback lens ("every action must produce a response — a sound, a color change, a number moving"): most of this is in place — `Act2Keypad.jsx` has shake + flash + sound on wrong input, `BiasCard.jsx` and `level3/Reveal.jsx` have staggered flip-in reveals with sound. The gap is the Typewriter component (`Typewriter.jsx`) itself: it has no skip/fast-forward control anywhere it's used. On a replay, or for an impatient expo visitor, being forced to sit through a full character-by-character type-out every single time (no click-to-skip, no speed variance) works against tension rather than for it — the design blueprint's own urgency language ("faster than comfortable") suggests speed should escalate, not stay fixed at whatever `speed` prop was set once.

## Minor codebase hygiene (not user-facing, but worth a pass)

`src/screens/Title.jsx`, `EndStub.jsx`, and `CompletionScreen.jsx` are orphaned — nothing imports them since `App.jsx` was rewritten around the shell (confirmed via search). `CompletionScreen.jsx` also has a hardcoded `0 / 1` in its "scenarios survived" stat (line 30) that ignores the actual result — dead code, but worth deleting rather than leaving it to be accidentally wired back in later.

## Suggested priority order

Fix the reveal-screen and locked-card contrast first — it's quantified, it's on the game's most important screen, and it's a small palette change. Then wire the `DispatcherCall` screen-reader-announcement pattern into the remaining stage transitions and give the scrambled keypad's buttons a focus ring — both are small, mechanical fixes to something you already built correctly once. The `prefers-reduced-motion` support is a similarly contained fix. The visual-craft gap (real sprites instead of color blocks) is the one item here that's a genuine scope decision rather than a quick fix, but it's also the one most likely to change "not engaging" feedback, so it's worth weighing against whatever time you have left before Levels 1–2 need building.
